I am going to be honest, when I first started looking for a job, my pride would not let me apply to anything less than the best brand-name companies, the highest paying roles, and the hardest, most competitive positions to get into.

Sooner or later, I realized that not only was this ineffective, but I genuinely DIDN’T know what I wanted to do in life, who I was, or what skills I actually had.

To successfully land a job, you need to essentially convince the employer that you are genuinely interested in the industry and the you can bring value to the role that you’re trying to get into.

It’s really that simple. THAT IS IT.

However, when you try to apply for roles that you have no EVIDENCE of interest in, no projects for, and no actual knowledge about, it will show. It shows in your interviews, in your CV, and even in the type of personality you come across as.

It’s easy to WANT to get into finance. It’s easy to WANT to get into the top consulting firms.

However, showing that you are a capable person that can work in Finance, and that you have the ABILITY to actually make it in those industries, is a completely different thing.

Truthfully, the way to successfully make it past interviews is by understanding who you are and what you can actually bring to the table, and not half-assing it or lying your way through it.

When I first wanted to get into finance, I tried to learn as much as possible about macro trends, the right personality for finance, and what it meant to be a numbers guy.

But looking back, I think I was more obsessed with the idea of finance than actually understanding whether it fit me. I liked the prestige, the image, and what it represented. But that is very different from having real interest, real proof, and real ability.

That was probably the biggest shift for me. I stopped asking myself, “what is the most impressive job I can get?” and started asking, “what am I actually good at, what do I actually enjoy, and what can I back up with evidence?”

Because at the end of the day, employers can tell. They can tell when you are forcing it. They can tell when you are saying what you think they want to hear. And they can also tell when you genuinely understand the role, the industry, and yourself.

That is what helped me the most. Not trying to become this perfect candidate for every role, but becoming more honest about what I was actually interested in and building from there.

The biggest mistake I made at the start was thinking that wanting something badly was enough. It isn’t. Wanting to get into a competitive industry means nothing on its own. A lot of people want it and there's a huge chance that they're much more capable than you, so don't try to be the best, just try to be yourself.

What matters is whether you can show proof. Proof that you’ve taken the time to learn. Proof that you’ve explored the space. Proof that your interest is not just talk. And proof that you actually have something to offer.

As an international student from a non-target university, you already have enough stacked against you. So you cannot afford to be vague, performative, or unrealistic. You need to know your story, know your strengths, and be honest about where you fit.

That, more than anything else, is what helped me get a graduate job in the UK.
