Just a disclaimer before you read this article, this isn’t meant to bash academics at all, but more just a reality check that being academic-smart is its own thing, and getting jobs is also its own thing.

For me, when I was applying to jobs, I was really scared to even start because I didn’t have a strong academic background. My grades were meh, and the university I came from didn’t really have a strong brand name either.

But along the journey of applying, speaking to recruiters, and slowly understanding how the job-finding process actually works, I realised that just like being good at exams or doing well in a course, there is also a certain set of conditions, rules, and things that employers look for when deciding whether to give you a job.

And I think that’s really my main point.

Being good at academics is one thing, but being employable is a completely different thing, and those two, in my opinion, do not always correlate as much as people think they do.

Maybe unless you’re trying to get into a super technical-heavy role, where you genuinely need that background knowledge and expertise, then yes, 100%, your degree, what you learned, and how well you did in it will probably matter a lot more.

But even then, I still believe you can be the most academically smart person and still not be able to land a role.

Because getting a job is not just about being intelligent in a classroom setting. It’s also about knowing how to position yourself, how to communicate your value, how to show relevant experience, how to make an employer believe that you actually understand the role and can do it.

And to be totally real, a lot of these things matter most when you’re trying to get into the very top companies. Even then, though, it’s not the end of the world if you don’t have perfect grades.

I was still able to get past a lot of first stages with a high 2:1, mainly because I made sure the experience I did have was relevant enough and made sense for the roles I was applying to.

So I think that was one of the biggest things I learned.

Not having the best grades does not automatically disqualify you. And not being the most academic person does not mean you cannot be employable.

They are just two different games, and a lot of people confuse one for the other.
